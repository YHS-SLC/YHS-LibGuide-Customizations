6. "Searching Like a Pro" (Strategic Exploration)

There are many tools to search for sources, ranging from a simple google search to complex database queries. Here's how to level up your search game regardless of format!

Planning Your Search

  1. Topic Statement

    • Write out exactly what you're looking for
    • Example: "How does social media affect teenage mental health?"

  2. Keyword Mining

    • Break your topic into main concepts
    • Example: "social media" + "teenagers" + "mental health"
    • List synonyms for each concept:
      • social media → "social networks", "Facebook", "Instagram", "TikTok"
      • teenagers → "teens", "adolescents", "young adults"
      • mental health → "psychological wellbeing", "anxiety", "depression"

Search Strategies

  1. Boolean Magic (Using AND, OR, NOT)

    • AND narrows your search: "teenagers" AND "social media" AND "anxiety"
    • OR broadens with alternatives: ("teenagers" OR "adolescents")
    • NOT excludes: "mental health" NOT "adults"

  2. Power Moves

    • Use quotation marks for exact phrases: "social media addiction"
    • Try wildcards: teen* finds teen, teens, teenager, teenagers
    • Use parentheses to group concepts: ("social media" OR "social networks") AND (teen* OR adolescen*)

Where to Search

  1. Start Broad

    • Google Scholar for a quick overview
    • Wikipedia to learn key terms (check their references!)

  2. Go Deep

    • Library databases for academic articles
    • Government websites (.gov) for statistics
    • Educational sites (.edu) for research

  3. Get Specific

    • Subject-specific databases
    • Professional organizations
    • Academic journals in your field

Search Refinement Loop

  1. Try a Search

    • Start with your basic keywords

  2. Evaluate Results

    • Too many? Add more specific terms
    • Too few? Try broader terms or synonyms
    • Off-topic? Revise your keywords

  3. Adjust and Repeat

    • Use what you learn to improve your next search
    • Look for new keywords in good results
    • Try different combinations

Pro Tips

  • Keep track of successful search combinations
  • Save good sources as you find them
  • Use the "cited by" feature in Google Scholar to find newer related articles
  • Check out "related articles" suggestions
  • Ask librarians about specialized databases for your topic

Remember: Good searching is like a conversation - you'll get better results if you're willing to adjust your approach based on what you find!
